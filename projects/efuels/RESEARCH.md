# Evidence dossier: PyPSA parametrisation for a Whyalla e-methanol-to-diesel facility

## Executive summary and recommended base case

A Whyalla facility sized to substitute the diesel output of Ampol Lytton or Viva Energy Geelong is a **GW-class, multi-billion-dollar undertaking that sits one order of magnitude above any e-methanol plant operating or under construction today**. Kassø — the global reference plant at 52 MW electrolyser and ~32–42 kt/y methanol — would need to be replicated approximately **140–170 times in methanol capacity** to match a single Australian refinery's diesel output after MTG/MOGD conversion losses. The modelling should adopt the following base case: **~6 GW installed PEM/alkaline electrolyser, ~25–35 TWh/y renewable input, ~2.5 Mt/y methanol intermediate, ~1.0 Mt/y synthetic diesel via MTO+MOGD or FT, ~3.5 Mt/y CO₂ demand**. At this scale the project is not technology-limited — every unit operation has commercial precedent — but is **economically stranded without ~AUD 800–1,500/t carbon-price equivalent revenue support**, a cost gap that even the Hydrogen Headstart (AUD 2 billion for ~1 GW) and the $2/kg Hydrogen Production Tax Incentive cannot bridge without CfD-style offtake underwriting.

The most consequential parametric uncertainty is **CO₂ feedstock**: local biogenic supply (wineries, biogas) can provide at most ~20 kt/y against a ~3.5 Mt/y requirement, forcing reliance on DAC at AUD 400–900/t (2030) or on transitional Whyalla Steelworks natural-gas DRI flue gas (~2 Mt/y during transition). The second-most consequential is **electrolyser load factor**: Kassø's implied 41–49% LHV electricity-to-methanol efficiency falls below the 50–55% textbook value, and a solar-heavy Whyalla supply mix without firm backup will suffer similarly. The Ørsted FlagshipONE cancellation (August 2024) — a 70 MW e-methanol project abandoned after FID despite committed EU and Swedish funding — is the most important cautionary datum: **project CAPEX inflation and the inability to sign long-term offtake at sustainable pricing** were decisive, not technology risk.

## Scaling mathematics: from Lytton/Geelong diesel to Whyalla GW-scale

**Australian refinery context.** Only two refineries remain. Lytton (Ampol, Brisbane, 109,000 bpd nameplate, NCI 6.55) produced **5.2 GL in FY2024** across petrol, diesel, jet, LPG; Geelong (Viva, 120,000 bpd nameplate, 1954 vintage) supplies ~50% of Victoria's fuel and ~10% of Australia's fuel, with FY2025 intake of 37.1 Mbbl. Both hold Fuel Security Services Payments (FSSP); the Federal Government in March 2026 extended FSSP support to 2030 with optionality for conversion to import terminals. **Diesel typically represents 40–50% of Australian refinery output**, implying **~2.1–2.5 GL/y (~1.75–2.1 Mt/y) diesel-equivalent substitution target** for either refinery at current output levels. Note the target is already smaller than nameplate because both refineries operate ~70–85% availability; at nameplate the target is ~2.6–3.0 GL/y.

**Stoichiometric chain for PyPSA.** From diesel back to electricity, using literature-validated conversion factors:

| Step | Ratio | Source |
|---|---|---|
| t diesel → t methanol (MTO+MOGD route, distillate-selective) | 2.8–3.2 t MeOH/t diesel | MDPI Ruokonen 2021 TEA; 45% diesel + 27% jet + 17% gasoline yield |
| t diesel → t methanol (MTG then hydrocracking heavy cut) | 2.4–2.5 t MeOH/t hydrocarbon (gasoline-dominant; poor diesel match) | ExxonMobil/AWOE |
| t methanol → t H₂ | 0.19–0.20 t H₂/t MeOH | Stoichiometric (3 H₂ : CO₂ : MeOH) |
| t methanol → t CO₂ | 1.37–1.45 t CO₂/t MeOH | Stoichiometric + vent losses |
| MWh → t H₂ (alkaline, 2026) | 50–55 MWh/t H₂ | IRENA, IEA, CSIRO |
| MWh → t H₂ (PEM, 2026) | 48–52 MWh/t H₂ | same |

**Base-case build-up for 2 Mt/y diesel output.** Using MTO+MOGD at 3.0 t MeOH/t diesel: **6 Mt/y methanol**, requiring **1.14 Mt/y H₂**, **8.3 Mt/y CO₂**, and **~57 TWh/y** electricity at 50 MWh/t H₂. For a 3,500 FLH solar-heavy profile, this requires **~16 GW installed electrolyser** — which is clearly infeasible at current technology readiness. **The practical model target should be scaled down to 1 Mt/y diesel (~50% Lytton/Geelong diesel substitution)**: 3 Mt/y MeOH, 0.57 Mt/y H₂, 4.2 Mt/y CO₂, ~28 TWh/y electricity, ~8 GW electrolyser at 3,500 FLH or ~4 GW at 7,000 FLH firmed. **For modelling purposes, the recommended "headline" plant is 5–6 GW installed electrolyser capacity, which matches exactly the scale range originally assumed in the user's brief.**

## Kassø benchmark: the primary reference plant

European Energy's Kassø plant (Aabenraa, Southern Jutland) inaugurated **13 May 2025** after first raw methanol production on **12 March 2025** — slipping approximately **18–20 months** from the original Siemens Energy schedule of "commercial methanol H2 2023." It is the **only operating commercial-scale e-methanol plant globally** and therefore the single most important data point for any Australian e-methanol techno-economic model.

**Plant configuration.** 52 MW Siemens Energy PEM electrolyser in 3 × 17.5 MW arrays; in-house REintegrate methanol synthesis reactor technology acquired by European Energy in late 2021; CO₂ supplied by ENVO Biogas Tønder (~30 km distant) via Ammongas biogas-upgrading CO₂ capture (EE subsidiary), with offtake for up to **60,000 t/y biogenic CO₂** against ~45,000 t/y actual flow. Electricity is sourced ~50% directly from the **adjacent 304 MW Kassø Solar Park** (largest in the Nordics) and ~50% from the Danish grid via a balancing agreement with Danske Commodities (Equinor subsidiary, operational since June 2024). Water consumption is **~90,000 t/y** (2.1–2.8 t water per t methanol). The plant exports **~50 GWh/y waste heat** to Aabenraa district heating.

**Capacity and efficiency.** Nameplate is **42,000 t/y methanol**, but European Energy's own IEA Bioenergy Task 39 presentation (December 2024) discloses realistic "clean methanol" output at **32,000 t/y** (nominal 42,000 t, with raw output 50,000 t before distillation losses). Hydrogen production is 6,000 t/y (EE) vs 8,000 t/y (H2 View, inauguration coverage). Annual electricity consumption is **360–380 GWh/y**, implying an overall electricity-to-methanol LHV efficiency of **~41–49%** — materially below the 50–55% value commonly assumed in TEAs, and a critical downward adjustment for Australian modelling.

**CAPEX.** The only public figure is **~€150 million (~A$248 million)** reported by Marine Insight (May 2025); European Energy has not published an audited breakdown. This implies **~€2.9 m/MW electrolyser installed** and **~€3,570–€4,690 per t/y MeOH capacity** depending on which output figure is used. Financing was structured as a **€53 million Danish Green Investment Fund loan (October 2022)** co-financed by Jyske Bank, with a further **green-financing bridge facility (Nordea + SMBC with EIFO partial guarantee) closed January 2026** to carry operations pending project-finance takeout. The EU Innovation Fund's €55.8 million grant awarded October 2024 is for European Energy's **next-generation 100 kt plant**, not Kassø itself — a frequently misreported fact. **No EIB direct loan** is on record. Mitsui holds 49% of the project SPV (acquired September 2023) alongside EE's 51%.

**Offtake.** Contracts span Maersk (10,000 t/y originally, expanded MoU up to 300,000 t/y across EE's broader portfolio; first 1,000 t bunker delivered May 2025 to *Laura Mærsk*), LEGO (POM plastics feedstock), Novo Nordisk (medical device plastics), and Circle K Danmark (legacy Reintegrate road-fuel agreement, ~50 ML over 5 years). **Prices are not disclosed.** S&P Global estimates e-methanol spot at **US$1,200–1,600/t** against Methanex fossil methanol Q4-2025 contracts at US$340–800/t — a **2–4× premium** that European Energy's CEO expects to close by ~2035. The plant achieved **ISCC EU RFNBO certification in April 2025** (first-ever for methanol) confirming 97% lifecycle GHG reduction.

**Transferable benchmarks for Whyalla.** Scaling Kassø linearly gives headline unit costs of **~A$4.8 million per MW electrolyser all-in** and **~A$6,000/t·y methanol capacity** (excluding CO₂ capture capex borne by Ammongas/ENVO). These are materially higher than vendor brochures for GW-scale projects suggest is achievable but are the only audit-relevant operational data point.

## Methanol-to-fuels conversion pathway

**MTG is fundamentally a gasoline process.** ExxonMobil fixed-bed MTG over ZSM-5 delivers ~0.40 kg gasoline per kg methanol with ~85–88% C5+ in the hydrocarbon cut and 10–15% LPG. Energetic efficiency from methanol to gasoline is very high (~90–93% LHV) because the process is near-thermoneutral after methanol-to-DME equilibration heat is recovered. TIGAS (Topsoe) is similar at 77–81 wt% C5+ gasoline. Commercial references are Motunui NZ (14,500 bpd, decommissioned 1990s), Shanxi JAMG (~2,500 bpd, RMB 1.7 B ≈ US$249 M, 2009), and **Ashgabat TIGAS Turkmenistan** (15,500 bpd from natural gas via methanol, US$1.7 B EPC by Kawasaki/Rönesans, operational 2019) — giving the best-anchored CAPEX intensity at **~US$110,000 per daily barrel of gasoline output**. Around 11 Chinese MTG units were online by 2017 (EIA).

**For diesel-maximised operation, the MTO+MOGD pathway is preferred** but lacks recent commercial reference. UOP/Norsk Hydro MTO (SAPO-34 catalyst) and DMTO (DICP/Sinopec, ~13 Mt/y olefins across 20 DMTO units in China as of 2024) reliably deliver C2/C3 olefins from methanol. **Mobil MOGD** then oligomerises over ZSM-5 at 200–300 °C, 40–105 bar to **82% distillate / 15% gasoline / 3% light gases** with liquid-fuel yields up to 95 wt% — tunable across the entire 0/100 to 90/10 distillate/gasoline range. The pathway was demonstrated pre-commercially in a Mobil refinery but **has no modern commercial reference**, making CAPEX projection speculative. **Ruokonen et al. (MDPI 2021)** is the best public TEA: methanol-to-fuels total production cost **€3,409/t fuels (€273/MWh LHV)** with product split 17% gasoline, 27% kerosene, 45% diesel, balance LPG. **Methanol feedstock accounts for ~€963/t of the total cost** — i.e. methanol economics dominate.

**Fischer-Tropsch remains the cleanest diesel route.** e-FT (reverse water-gas-shift + cobalt or iron FT) bypasses methanol and delivers ~65–75% middle distillate after hydrocracking. ANL/Zang et al. (2021) finds e-FT diesel cost-competitive with US$3.1/gal petroleum diesel only if **H₂ ≤ US$0.80/kg** plus a CO₂ credit — a threshold no Australian project will meet this decade. For modelling, **both MTO+MOGD and FT should be run as alternative conversion blocks** with FT favoured if the model permits syngas-route direct CO₂ + H₂ → hydrocarbons.

**CAPEX reference points for the conversion step.** TIGAS at ~US$110,000/bpd gasoline; MTG incremental CAPEX +17–30% over an upstream methanol plant; Nacero Penwell planned at US$6.5–10 B for 70,000–100,000 bpd via TIGAS (now pivoting to SAF); HIF Matagorda at **US$6 B total capex** for 300 kt/y H₂ + 2 Mt/y recycled CO₂ → 1.4 Mt/y methanol → 200 Mgal/y (~750 ML/y) e-gasoline, with 1.8 GW electrolyser (originally Siemens, **switched to Electric Hydrogen September 2025**) and FID not yet taken as of April 2026. All these figures remain **pre-FID company projections**.

**Mass balance for PyPSA (recommended values).** Per tonne synthetic diesel: **3.0 t methanol, 0.57 t H₂, 4.15 t CO₂, ~28 MWh renewable electricity, ~2.5 t water**. Overall power-to-diesel LHV efficiency **~40% at nameplate, 35% realistic** factoring Kassø-style derating.

## Whyalla site-specific parametrisation

**Renewable resource is exceptional.** Bungala Solar (220 MW AC/275 MW DC, single-axis tracking, operating since 2018) achieves **~25% AC capacity factor** with ~570 GWh/y output — one of the highest AC CFs in the NEM. Port Augusta Renewable Energy Park (DP Energy/Iberdrola, 317 MW Stage 1, Stage 2 approved for +500 MW solar + 400 MW/3000 MWs synchronous condensers) shows **hybrid solar 25–27% CF, wind 35–40% CF**. Lincoln Gap Wind Farm (~300 MW) is consistent with **wind CF 35–45%** across the Eyre Peninsula. **Thermal winds peak afternoon/evening, complementing solar** — the single most valuable native feature of the site for electrolyser load-factor firming.

**Grid capacity.** ElectraNet's **Davenport 275 kV substation** is the regional hub. **Project EnergyConnect** (800 MW NSW–SA interconnector, Stage 1 energised April 2025, Stage 2 completion targeted last quarter 2026) unlocks 5.3 GW of new renewables into SA/NSW, but is **not sized to host a 6 GW dedicated industrial load** — any GW-class electrolyser at Whyalla must be supplied primarily from behind-the-meter generation, with grid connection used only for balancing. **Cultana Pumped Hydro (225 MW / 1,770 MWh, EnergyAustralia, seawater) is effectively cancelled** after failing to secure a Defence Department land lease, so firming must come from batteries and/or hydrogen buffer storage in the model.

**Land is available and already assembled.** The now-deferred Hydrogen Jobs Plan secured options for **238 ha in the Whyalla Industrial Estate** (preferred electrolyser site), **8.7 ha in Cultana Industrial Estate** (hydrogen power station site), and additional adjacent land, all with Barngarla Determination Aboriginal Corporation engagement completed. Port Bonython has **~2,000 ha developable** on the Point Lowly peninsula, 16 km northeast of Whyalla.

**Water requires the Northern Water desalination project.** The Morgan-Whyalla pipeline (~25 GL/y, Murray-sourced) is already fully committed. BHP's once-approved Point Lowly desal (200–280 ML/day) was abandoned when the Olympic Dam expansion changed technology. The **SA Government's Northern Water Supply Project** (Mullaquana site confirmed December 2025, **260 ML/day ≈ 95 GL/y**, 400 km pipeline, **~A$5 billion CAPEX**, FID FY2026/27, first water 2029) is the only credible supply pathway at this scale, with BHP as foundation customer. Adelaide Desalination Plant benchmark opex implies a delivered water cost of **A$1.00–2.50/kL** at Whyalla after pipeline transport. At 2.5 t water/t methanol, water is a minor cost item (~A$2.5–6.3/t methanol).

**CO₂ sourcing is the binding constraint.** For a 1 Mt/y diesel plant requiring ~4 Mt/y CO₂:

| Source | Available volume (t CO₂/y) | 2026 cost (AUD/t) | 2030 cost | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SA wine industry (Barossa + McLaren Vale fermentation) | 8,000–12,000 | 80–250 | similar | Seasonal, micro-scale, negligible for GW plant |
| SA biogas / ethanol | \<5,000 | — | — | Negligible |
| Whyalla Steelworks (BF-BOF historic) | 3–4 Mt; ~2 Mt in interim NG-DRI phase | Integrated capture ~AUD 60–120/t | Drops to ~zero at full H₂-DRI | **Transition-window opportunity only**; site in administration since February 2025 |
| Adbri Birkenhead cement (Adelaide) | 1.0–1.5 Mt | 80–150 (post-combustion); 40–80 (LEILAC) | 30–60 (LEILAC scaled) | Stable, scalable, but **370 km from Whyalla** by sea |
| Nyrstar Port Pirie | 0.3–0.5 Mt | 60–120 | similar | Small scale; only 85 km from Whyalla |
| Santos Moomba CCS | 1.7 Mt captured | 30–50 | similar | Geologically sequestered; diversion possible but requires new pipeline ~659 km |
| DAC (1PointFive/Climeworks/Heirloom) | Unlimited | 1,000–1,500 spot, 300–450 contracted (Microsoft-type) | 375–900 | High-purity, future-proof, but **dominates LCOM**; no Australian 45Q equivalent |

**Recommendation: use a hybrid CO₂ portfolio in the base case** — ~50% Whyalla Steelworks DRI interim phase (2028–~2035) at AUD 80/t delivered; ~25% Port Pirie Nyrstar post-combustion at AUD 100/t; ~25% DAC at AUD 500/t (2030 central). Blended cost: **~AUD 195/t CO₂**, contributing **~AUD 285/t to methanol LCOM** and **~AUD 1,180/t to diesel LCOF**. Sensitivity with 100% DAC at AUD 500/t adds another AUD 900/t diesel. This is the **single most consequential cost sensitivity in the model**.

**Port Bonython infrastructure is already substantial.** Santos operates the 2.4 km wharf (capesize to 110,000 DWT), a fractionation plant receiving Cooper Basin liquids via the 659 km Moomba pipeline, and Port Bonython Fuels (Mitsubishi/Petro Diamond since 2016) hosts **81 ML diesel import storage** with ~1 B L/y throughput. The **A$100 M federal-state Port Bonython Hydrogen Hub** (A$70 M Commonwealth, A$30 M SA, finalised October 2023) funds common-user wharf upgrades, pipelines, and storage. The 2,000 ha of developable land, deep-water access, and existing fuels storage make Port Bonython **the single most credible export/domestic shipping outlet for any Whyalla e-fuels plant**.

**H2U Eyre Peninsula Gateway FEED is archived.** The Demonstrator Stage (75 MW electrolyser, 120 t/day ammonia, Worley FEED awarded March 2021, Casale engineering November 2022, RWE offtake MoU, ~A$240 M project cost) is listed as "Archived" on the CSIRO HyResource database. The broader SA hydrogen ecosystem was dismantled in **February 2025** when the Malinauskas government placed Whyalla Steelworks into administration (KordaMentha), scrapped the Office of Hydrogen Power, and redirected the A$593 M Hydrogen Jobs Plan funding into the A$2.4 B joint federal-state bailout of the steelworks. GE Vernova turbine contract (200 MW H₂ power) and the 250 MW electrolyser order are **deferred indefinitely**. For the user's PyPSA model, this means **there is no live brownfield FEED or EPC foundation to leverage**, but the Port Bonython hub infrastructure and the Whyalla land options remain intact.

## Key techno-economic parameters for PyPSA

**Electrolyser CAPEX (AUD/kW installed, large-scale 100+ MW project).** Synthesising CSIRO GenCost 2024-25/2025-26, IEA Global Hydrogen Review 2024, IRENA Green Hydrogen Cost Reduction, Electric Hydrogen whitepaper 2024, NREL H2A, Krishnan et al., Reksten et al.:

| Technology | 2026 | 2030 | 2035 | Efficiency (MWh/t H₂) | Stack life (hrs) | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alkaline (AEL) | 1,200–1,800 | 700–1,100 | 500–800 | 52 → 48 | 60,000–90,000 | High |
| PEM | 1,500–2,400 | 900–1,400 | 600–950 | 50 → 46 | 50,000–80,000 | High |
| SOEC | 4,000–6,500 | 1,500–2,500 | 900–1,500 | 40 → 35 (with steam) | 20,000–40,000 | Low |

CSIRO GenCost 2025-26 draft (December 2025) projects **alkaline and PEM converging** with both approaching ~€300/kW (~A$500/kW) at large scale by 2030 per Reksten et al. 2022, while academic bottom-up stacks (Krishnan et al. 2023) indicate **PEM stack-only costs dropping from €384–1,071/kW (2020) to €63–234/kW (2030)**. NREL (May 2024) reports current installed PEM at ~US$2,000/kW for 1 MW systems, falling to ~US$500–700/kW at >100 MW. **Use AUD 1,500/kW (central), 1,000/kW (optimistic), 2,000/kW (conservative) for 2030 FID base case.** Stack replacement every 7–10 years at **30–40% of initial CAPEX**; degradation ~1%/year.

**Methanol synthesis CAPEX.** Methanex Geismar 3 greenfield reference **US$1.30 B for 1.8 Mt/y** = **US$720/t·y ≈ AUD 1,100/t·y**. Small-scale e-methanol (50 kt/y): US$100–150 M = US$2,000–3,000/t·y, reflecting poor economies of scale. CO₂-injection retrofits of existing methanol plants offer marginal CAPEX **~US$120/t·y** (Fairway Methanol LLC benchmark). Scaling: **use US$800/t·y (AUD 1,220/t·y) for a 3 Mt/y greenfield e-methanol synthesis island in 2030**, with Cu/ZnO/Al₂O₃ catalyst replacement every 2–5 years at US$20–40/kg.

**MTG/FT conversion CAPEX.** TIGAS Turkmenistan anchor: **US$110,000/bpd gasoline** = US$2,600/t·y for gasoline-spec product; scale to MTO+MOGD distillate **US$150,000–200,000/bpd diesel ≈ AUD 5,500–7,500/t·y** (speculative — use AUD 6,500/t·y central with ±40% range).

**Storage.** No SA salt caverns: compressed H₂ vessels at **AUD 800–1,500/kg capacity** (limiting scale — use methanol buffer instead). CO₂ liquid storage **AUD 300–500/t·y capacity**. Methanol atmospheric tanks **AUD 100–200/t·y** (leverage existing Port Bonython Fuels for logistics).

**OPEX.** Fixed O&M: **2.5–4.0% of CAPEX/year** for electrolyser; 3–5% for synthesis/conversion. Staffing: **150–250 FTE at AUD 170,000 loaded** = AUD 25–42 M/year. Variable: catalyst AUD 3–8/t methanol; maintenance AUD 4–10/MWh.

**Financial and market.** WACC 7–10% real pre-tax renewables; **10–13% real pre-tax for FOAK hydrogen/e-fuels** (CEFC internal benchmark range). Project life 25 years. **Australian Safeguard Mechanism baseline 2026: AUD 35–40/t CO₂e, ACCU forward curve rising to AUD 60–80 by 2030** (Reputex/Jarden). **IMO MEPC 83 Net-Zero Framework** takes effect March 2027 with penalties from 2028: **US$100/t CO₂eq Tier 1 RU** and **US$380/t Tier 2 RU** on emissions above targets — **this creates a credit value for e-methanol bunker fuel of approximately US$480/t CO₂eq avoided (AUD 735/t), equating to ~AUD 2,200/t methanol premium over fossil bunker** if the vessel is selling Surplus Units.

**PPA pricing in SA.** CSIRO GenCost 2025-26 (December 2025): **NEM delivered LCOE AUD 91/MWh (generation-only AUD 81/MWh)** at the 82% renewables target for 2030; current delivered AUD 129/MWh. For SA specifically, firmed solar+wind+battery PPAs **AUD 80–110/MWh** (2025 signings, BRC-A tracker); unfirmed solar **AUD 45–70/MWh**; unfirmed wind **AUD 60–85/MWh**. Australian corporate PPA market was ~8 TWh in 2024–25, nearly double prior records. **Use AUD 55/MWh unfirmed solar + AUD 70/MWh unfirmed wind + AUD 140/MWh battery-firmed top-up for the load factor block in 2030 base case**, weighted to target ~5,500 FLH electrolyser load factor.

## Policy stack and offtake economics

The Australian policy environment provides **three stacked revenue supports** relevant to the model:

**Hydrogen Headstart Program** has been **doubled to AUD 4 billion total** (AUD 2 B original 2023-24 budget + AUD 2 B added 2024-25 budget). Round 1 was awarded in 2025 to **Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners (AUD 814 million for 1.5 GW development)** under a contracts-for-difference structure delivering hydrogen production credits over 10 years from ~2027. Round 1 shortlist included six projects totalling 3.5+ GW: bp H2Kwinana (105 MW, SAF eligibility relevant to this model), HIF Asia Pacific Tasmania eFuel (144 MW), KEPCO Port of Newcastle (750 MW), Stanwell Central Queensland (720 MW), Origin H2U, and CIP. **Round 2 EOI opened October 2025** with AUD 1.3 B allocated over 2024-25 to 2033-34. **Eligible e-fuels derivative projects (including e-methanol for shipping) qualify** — this is the most important single funding mechanism available to a Whyalla project.

**Hydrogen Production Tax Incentive** (Future Made in Australia Act, passed Senate 10 February 2025): **AUD 2/kg refundable tax offset** for renewable hydrogen produced from 2027-28 to 2039-40, up to 10 years per project. Eligibility requires 100% renewable-powered electrolysis (matching the Hydrogen Headstart standard). For a Whyalla plant at 0.57 Mt/y H₂, this is worth **AUD 1.14 billion/year** for 10 years — a material revenue stream, though not stackable with Headstart CfD in overlapping periods.

**Port Bonython Hydrogen Hub** federal-state A$100 M (A$70 M Commonwealth + A$30 M SA) remains intact post-February 2025; funds common-user infrastructure.

**Fuel quality.** MTG/MTO-MOGD/FT synthetic diesel meets Australian Automotive Diesel Determination (10 ppm S from December 2025, cetane ≥51) as drop-in product without blend-wall restriction — no regulatory barrier to domestic sale.

**Import parity diesel** Singapore MOPS + quality premium + freight yields Australian terminal gate prices of **AUD 1.10–1.40/L wholesale** through 2026 (pre-tax, pre-GST), rising to an estimated AUD 1.30–1.70/L by 2030 on central oil-price scenarios. At AUD 1.30/L wholesale diesel = **AUD 1,550/t** (density 0.84). Against the Ruokonen-implied LCOF of **€3,409/t ≈ AUD 5,600/t** for methanol-to-fuels, the **revenue gap is ~AUD 4,000/t diesel** — requiring IMO bunker premium plus Hydrogen Headstart plus HPTI plus Safeguard Mechanism credits plus export to EU ReFuelEU Aviation/FuelEU Maritime premium markets to close.

**EU demand signals** are the strongest exit option. **FuelEU Maritime** (effective January 2025) requires 2% GHG-intensity reduction 2025 → 6% 2030 → 14.5% 2035 → 80% 2050 on a well-to-wake basis with a 2008 baseline of 93.3 gCO₂e/MJ. **ReFuelEU Aviation** mandates SAF blending 2% 2025 → 6% 2030 (0.7% RFNBO sub-mandate) → 20% 2035 → 70% 2050. These create firm implied premiums of **EUR 800–1,500/t for RFNBO methanol** through 2030 in EU markets, supporting an Australian export case to Rotterdam/Hamburg.

## Comparable projects and lessons

**Ørsted FlagshipONE (Sweden) cancellation August 2024** is the single most important recent data point. The 70 MW electrolyser / 55 kt/y e-methanol project took FID in December 2022 after Ørsted acquired full ownership from Liquid Wind, with Carbon Clean contracted for 70 kt/y biogenic CO₂ capture from the Horneborgsverket CHP plant. Ørsted took a **DKK 1.5 B (US$221 M) impairment** plus **DKK 300 M cancellation provision**, against an original reported CAPEX of ~US$175 M. **Cited reasons (Mads Nipper statement):** "inability to sign long-term offtake contracts at sustainable pricing and significantly higher project costs." EU funding (Horizon Europe, EU-Catalyst programme) had not been fully disbursed at cancellation. **Liquid Wind has since re-initiated the project under FlagshipTHREE.**

**HIF Haru Oni (Chile)** is the only operating e-fuels plant beyond Kassø: 3.4 MW wind + PEM electrolysis + MTG producing 130,000 L/y e-gasoline to Porsche since December 2022. Expansion (Cabo Negro, Uruguay, Brazil) under development but no FIDs.

**HIF Matagorda (Texas, USA)** is the only GW-scale e-methanol project near FID globally: **US$6 B CAPEX target**, 1.8 GW electrolyser (Siemens Energy until Electric Hydrogen was selected in September 2025, 300 kt/y H₂, 2 Mt/y recycled CO₂, 1.4 Mt/y e-methanol refined via TIGAS to ~200 Mgal/y (~750 ML/y) e-gasoline. **Construction originally 2023, first production 2026** — both slipped; FID not yet taken as of April 2026. Bechtel engaged for FEED.

**Infinium Roadrunner (West Texas)** FT-based with Amazon, American Airlines, IAG offtakes; in construction but limited public detail.

**Australian comparables.** Western Green Energy Hub (WA, 50 GW aspirational but pre-FEED), HyEnergy (Province Resources WA), Yara Pilbara green ammonia (scaled back), HIF Tasmania eFuel Facility (144 MW, shortlisted Hydrogen Headstart Round 1), and the **Ampol Lytton Brisbane Renewable Fuels pre-FEED** (July 2024 MoU with GrainCorp and IFM Investors for **up to 750 ML/y HEFA SAF+renewable diesel at Lytton**, ARENA co-funded) — a **competing bio-route decarbonisation pathway** that targets the same refinery but uses canola/UCO/tallow rather than e-fuels.

## Data gaps requiring further investigation

1. **Kassø CAPEX breakdown.** The headline €150 M figure is from secondary trade press. European Energy has not published an audited split between electrolyser, synthesis, BoP, CO₂ capture, and utilities. Recommend direct enquiry via EE investor relations for a redacted cost curve.
2. **Kassø actual load factor and 2025–26 production.** Only production milestones (first methanol March 2025, inauguration May 2025, bridge financing January 2026) are published; no volumetric run data. This is the most consequential operational data point for modelling.
3. **HIF Matagorda updated CAPEX post Electric Hydrogen switch.** Electrolyser change of supplier typically ±15–25% on that line item.
4. **Northern Water delivered cost to Whyalla industrial customers.** AUD 5 B project CAPEX is published but allocated tariff to secondary customers (beyond BHP foundation offtake) has not been disclosed.
5. **Whyalla Steelworks CO₂ supply contractability during DRI transition.** KordaMentha administration and uncertain ownership (Greensteel Australia vs others) makes medium-term CO₂ availability uncertain; a CO₂ offtake MoU is not currently marketable.
6. **Port Bonython liquid CO₂ import infrastructure cost.** No published estimate for cryogenic tank farm + jetty upgrade to handle LCO₂ imports.
7. **Hydrogen Headstart Round 2 e-fuels eligibility details.** Guidelines under revision as of April 2026; applicability to a Whyalla MTO+MOGD project (with H₂ as intermediate rather than final product) requires ARENA/DCCEEW clarification.
8. **MTO+MOGD vendor CAPEX quotes.** No modern commercial MOGD plant exists; CAPEX projections rely on 1980s Mobil pilot data escalated to 2026 dollars and should be tested via Honeywell UOP or Topsoe budgetary quotations.
9. **ACCU forward curves specific to synthetic fuel fuel-switching.** Safeguard Mechanism applicability to an exporter producing e-fuel (not emitting fossil CO₂) is legally ambiguous under current CER guidance.
10. **Whyalla seawater quality for electrolyser feed.** Upper Spencer Gulf seawater has elevated salinity (~40 g/L vs ocean 35 g/L) which affects desalination energy intensity.

## Conclusion: parameter envelope and headline verdict

At the most probable base-case parameters for a 2030 FID, **delivered LCOF for Whyalla synthetic diesel lands in the AUD 4,500–6,500/t range** (AUD 3.80–5.50/L), against an Australian wholesale diesel import parity of AUD 1,500–1,800/t. **Closing this ~AUD 3,000–4,500/t gap requires**, stacked: IMO Net-Zero Framework credits (~AUD 700–2,200/t diesel equivalent by 2030–35), Hydrogen Headstart CfD (~AUD 500–1,200/t), HPTI ($2/kg = ~AUD 1,140/t diesel), EU export premium (~AUD 1,200–2,500/t for RFNBO diesel), and an implicit Safeguard Mechanism crediting mechanism not yet legislated. **Only in the favourable stacking case does the project clear hurdle rate**, which explains why every comparable project worldwide (FlagshipONE, HIF Matagorda, Nacero Penwell, H2U Eyre Peninsula) has either cancelled, deferred, or remained pre-FID despite robust underlying fundamentals.

The Whyalla site itself is **technically superior** to Kassø (higher solar and wind capacity factors, co-located industrial land, planned desalination, deep-water port) and to FlagshipONE (larger renewable resource, better grid backbone post-PEC). The binding constraint is **neither geography nor technology** but **CO₂ feedstock strategy and revenue certainty**. A PyPSA model that solves this problem well should treat **CO₂ supply cost** and **electrolyser load factor** as the two most sensitive parameters, and should evaluate the MTO+MOGD route against e-FT on a common basis, since FT's higher distillate selectivity may outweigh its modestly higher conversion CAPEX when diesel is the target product. The single most consequential near-term input the user should pursue is a **redacted CAPEX breakdown from European Energy's Kassø SPV or Mitsui Tank Terminals** — because Kassø remains, uniquely, the only audited operating reference in the world for the process train the Whyalla model is trying to scale.
